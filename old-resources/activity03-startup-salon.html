Maker Party | Startup Salon

CC-BY-SA by Mozilla

45 minutes

Help local business leaders road-map business plans built on open platforms.

Activity 3 of 5

Activity Objectives

  • Participants will develop open practices for business.
  • Participants sign this petition for copyright reform.

Audience

  • Local business owners & entrepreneurs

Materials

  • Large 102mm x 152mm (4” x 6”) index cards
  • Stickers
  • Craft supplies (e.g. googly eyes, pom poms, glue)
  • Markers
  • Scissors
  • Hole-punch
  • Internet-connected computers
  • Projector
  • Screening surface
  • Optional: Circuit stickers (see Chibitronics), 3v coin batteries, and copper tape or graphite pencils

  • Preparation

    1. Review your planning and facilitation materials.
    2. Review this activity before you run it.
    3. Gather your materials.

  • Hack Your Nametag & Intros

    15 minutes

    Ask participants to create a nametag to wear during the event that captures an exhibit or performance project idea they have in support of common-sense copyright reform. Each participant can put their name and handle on one side and a question or project idea they have on the other. Then they can use your crafting materials to illustrate and/or illuminate either side or both sides with provided materials.

    With the next 5 minutes of the activity, participants go in a circle introducing themselves by sharing their names and questions or ideas with the group.

    Finally, introduce the campaign with this explainer video from Mozilla [placeholder].

  • Startup Salon

    40 minutes

    Facilitate a panel with 3 invited speakers from the local community who each take 10 minutes to share their experiences with copyright issues and working open. Invite professionals with experience in creative and technical fields that encounter copyright issues such as

    • Artists.
    • Coders.
    • Graphic artists.
    • Innovative entrepreneurs.
    • Journalists.
    • Publishers.
    • Scientists.
    • Software developers.

    Ask the speakers to describe the problems they or their customers/users face related to restrictive or outdated copyright law and proprietary practices and to explain how they solve those problems with open solutions (e.g. exploring the cost of MSFT contracts in the EU vs. open source solutions).


    Open source swiss knife, CC-BY-SA 3.0 by Johannes Spielhagen

    Moderate a 15-minute Q&A after the panelists speak so audience members can share their questions and concerns and hear panelists’ advice on how to proceed.

    If possible, find a way to share the content live, in real-time either through Facebook Live or Periscope. Encourage discussion through social media. If there is a local Mozilla social platform you want to use let us know and we can try to facilitate an introduction. You can also share highlights to social media using the #makerparty hashtag.

  • Hack a Business Plan

    40 minutes

    Drawing from the Mozilla Open Leadership Training Series (OLTS) exercises on stating your vision and using Open Canvas for planning, invite attendees to hack on business plans that use open resources and practices to solve or circumvent problems posed by outdated copyright laws. If your panelists can stay, ask them to mentor attendees as they develop their business plans.


    Business Model Canvas, CC-BY-SA 1.0 by Business Model Alchemist

    Take about 10 minutes to walk attendees through the canvas template you’ll use and give them the balance of the time to work together, in small groups, or individually, to draft their vision statements and business plans.

    Throughout the activity, encourage participants to share some of their experiences with hacking open business plans on social media using the #makerparty hashtag.

  • Reflection

    5 minutes

    Facilitate a brief reflective discussion at the end of your event using prompts like these:

    • In your own words, what are the advantages and disadvantages to building your business on open platforms or using open resources?
    • How do you think using open resources or offering an open product or service will impact customer relations or cost structures?
    • Is there an ideal state between open and proprietary business practices? Can you describe it?
    • How would it impact your business plan if different countries treated the resources you use and the products or services you offer differently under copyright law? For example, what would happen if a government in one country insisted that you use copyright protected works or proprietary software, rather than open licensing?
    • How would it impact your business if governments in different countries held different beliefs about what open resources you could use?
