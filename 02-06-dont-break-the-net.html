Maker Party Activities | Don't Break the Internet

CC-BY-SA by Mozilla

45 minutes

Prototype and share apps, games, and services that show people what's at stake if we let unreasonable copyright reform break the 'net and make it dangerous to link, quote, or remix.

Workshop Objectives

  • Help others understand the benefits of reasonable copyright reform that that they might take for granted.
  • Help others understand the benefits of FOSS that they might take for granted.
  • Create an app, game, or service that illustrates what's at stake with unreasonable copyright reform.

Audience

  • Internet consumers
  • Internet producers

Materials

  • Large 102mm x 152mm (4” x 6”) index cards
  • Stickers
  • Craft supplies (e.g. googly eyes, pom poms, glue)
  • Optional: Circuit stickers (see Chibitronics), 3v coin batteries, and copper tape or graphite pencils
  • Markers
  • Scissors
  • Hole-punch
  • Internet-connected computers
  • Projector
  • Prototyping materials
  • Screening surface

  • Before the Event

    1. Gather your event facilitators.
    2. Brief them on local copyright issues using campaign materials such as this “5 Points on Copyright Reform” document.
    3. Brief them on the campaign, its goals, and relevant KPIs.
    4. Decide on a purpose and audience for your event.
    5. Reach consensus on an agenda.
    6. Plan the event in detail.
    7. Secure the resources you need for the event, including materials and space.

  • Hack Your Nametag & Intros

    15 minutes

    Ask participants to create a nametag to wear during the event that captures an exhibit or performance project idea they have in support of common-sense copyright reform. Each participant can put their name and handle on one side and their project idea on the other, and illustrate or illuminate either side or both sides with provided materials.

    With the next 5 minutes of the activity, participants go in a circle introducing themselves by sharing their names and ideas with the group.

    Finally, introduce the campaign with this explainer video from Mozilla [placeholder].

  • Don't Break the Internet

    40 minutes

    The big idea here is to help technologists find ways to demonstrate the importance of FOSS and reasonable copyright laws through interactive experiences online. You and your participants should use this time to pitch ideas for websites, apps, and games that show thing like:

    • What it looks like when links go dark.
    • What it looks like when popular services break because F/OSS resources are under attack or taken down at the request of corporations selling competing, proprietary products.
    • What it looks like when open solutions solve copyright-connected problems.

    Projects like this might include:

    • anti.blog - a reader view add-on that lets you read content without links or linked or embedded media.
    • badMail - an add-on that redacts all mention of potentially copyrighted materials in your email and/or social media posts.
    • untextr - a texting service that doesn’t let you share media or links in sms messages.
    • It Came from Beyond Copyright - a pixel adventure game about open values or something like a fork of Fight for the Open Web!, a game built inside Minecraft that teaches about the open web.

    This first part of the workshop should be dedicated to:

    1. Pitching ideas. (10 minutes)
    2. Setting up repos. (5-10 minutes)
    3. Making or collecting assets or prototyping features. (30 minutes)

  • Break

    10 minutes

    Provide light refreshment according to local custom.

  • Workshop Time

    40 minutes

    Participants should take the first 30 minutes of this block of time to keep advancing their project ideas.

    With the last 10 minutes or so, facilitate a share-out of each project, its features, goals, features, progress, and next steps.

  • Closing

    10 minutes

    Facilitate a brief “I need/I can offer” exit poll with the group. Use a prompt like this to have participants reflect on the event, campaign, and copyright in general:

    To continue developing my app, I need _____ . To help you develop yours, I can offer _____.

    After everyone has had the chance to speak, thank attendees and share

    1. The CTA to sign the petition asking for common-sense copyright law.
    2. The CTA to pledge to get 5 others to sign it.
    3. A calendar for events coming out of this workshop.
    4. Ways to stay involved in the campaign and support one another’s events.
    5. Take-home Maker Party kits.
    6. Copyright cupcakes.

  • After the Event

    1. Gather facilitators for a debrief.
    2. Takes notes on what worked, what didn’t work, and what you would change.
    3. Review CTA results such as petition signatures to contribute data used for measurement against campaign KPIs.
    4. Photograph or otherwise document media produced by participants.
    5. Complete an event report and upload photos and other event documentation.
